The NDIS Early Childhood Pathway Explained

A calm, parent-friendly guide to the NDIS early childhood pathway — who it's for, how the process works, and what supports may be available for your child.

Talk to Able Aide Back to Learn

What is the early childhood pathway?

The early childhood approach is designed to help young children with disability or developmental concerns access support as early as possible. The focus is on early intervention — providing the right support early so children can build confidence, participate and develop everyday skills.

Who is it for?

  • Children under around 9 years of age
  • Children with a diagnosed disability
  • Children with developmental delay or emerging concerns
  • Families who feel something isn't quite right and want support

Typical steps in the pathway

1. Noticing concerns

You, a teacher, GP or therapist notices differences in development, communication, behaviour or social interaction.

2. Talking to professionals

Families often start with a GP, paediatrician or maternal & child health nurse. They may recommend assessments or referral to an early childhood partner.

3. Assessment & eligibility

The NDIA considers whether your child may benefit from short-term early intervention supports or a longer-term NDIS plan.

4. Planning meeting

You'll discuss your child's strengths, daily routines, what's working and what's challenging, and goals for the next 12–24 months.

5. Starting supports

Therapy (OT, speech, psychology, physio), family-centred support at home, and community and early learning supports.

What supports can be included?

Occupational therapy

Sensory needs, routines, independence and fine motor skills.

Speech therapy

Communication, speech sounds and social interaction.

Psychology

Emotional wellbeing, behaviour and social skills.

Family coaching

Parent support, strategy training and practical home-based guidance.

Your role as a parent or caregiver

You are the expert in your child. Share your concerns honestly, ask questions when things are unclear, and speak up if something doesn't feel right. Able Aide walks alongside families with warmth, clarity and realistic expectations.
