How to Prepare for an NDIS Plan Review

Plan reviews (reassessments) can feel stressful — but with the right preparation, they can also be an opportunity to get supports that better fit your life.

This guide walks through what to gather, what to think about and how to talk about your needs in a way that’s practical and clear.

Need help with a review coming up?

Able Aide can help you reflect on your current supports, gather information and feel more confident going into your NDIS plan review.

Support coordination Talk about your review

1. Understand what an NDIS plan review is

An NDIS plan review (now often called a “plan reassessment”) is when the NDIS looks at your current plan, goals and supports and decides what your next plan will look like. Sometimes this is a full review, other times it may be a light-touch check-in.

The key question they’re asking is: “Are your current supports helping you work towards your goals, or do things need to change?”

2. Look back: what has worked — and what hasn’t?

Before your plan review, it helps to reflect on the past 6–12 months. You don’t have to write an essay, but some simple notes can make a big difference.

Questions you can ask yourself (or talk through with a support coordinator):

  • Which supports helped you the most? (support workers, therapists, programs, equipment)
  • Were there supports you didn’t use — and why? (hard to access, wrong fit, no capacity)
  • Have there been changes in your health, living situation, family or mental health?
  • Did you feel like you had enough hours — or did things feel stretched?
  • Are you closer to your goals, or do they need to be updated?

Writing these down in dot points is enough. You can also use a simple planner or downloadable worksheets to keep everything in one place.

3. Gather reports and letters from your providers

The NDIS usually wants to see evidence from people who support you — like therapists, support coordinators, support workers or doctors. These reports help explain:

  • Why you need the support you’re asking for
  • What has changed since your last plan
  • What happens if supports are reduced

Common types of information that can help:

  • Occupational therapy, physio, speech or psychology reports
  • Behaviour support plans or updates
  • Letters from your GP or specialist
  • Progress summaries from your support coordinator or therapists
  • Examples of how supports help at home, in the community, work or school

It’s a good idea to request reports at least 4–6 weeks before your review where possible so providers have time to prepare them.

4. Be clear about your goals

Goals don’t need to sound fancy or complicated. They just need to describe what matters to you and what you’d like to be different or easier in the future.

Examples of everyday, practical goals:

  • “I want to shower and dress more independently with less support.”
  • “I want to feel confident going to appointments and doing my own shopping.”
  • “I want my child to join in more at kinder and manage routines at home.”
  • “I want to build a small support team around me that understands my anxiety.”

Support coordination, therapy and core supports can then be linked to these goals. This helps the NDIS see how funding connects to outcomes, not just tasks.

5. Think about what you need more of, less of, or different

Your next plan doesn’t have to look the same as your current one. Ask yourself:

  • Do I need more of something that is working?
  • Is there something I didn’t use that could be reduced or removed?
  • Do I need different supports — for example, more community access, or extra help at home?
  • Do I need help coordinating everything (Support Coordination)?

If you’re unsure, this is exactly where a Support Coordinator can help you think things through in a calm and structured way.

6. Plan what you’d like to say in the meeting

It’s completely normal to feel nervous in NDIS phone calls or meetings. Having a few key points written down can help you feel less overwhelmed.

You might write down:

  • 1–3 main changes since your last plan
  • 1–3 things that are still really hard
  • A few examples of how supports help in daily life
  • Any safety, behaviour or mental health concerns

You can also ask a support person to be with you — this could be a family member, trusted friend, support coordinator or advocate.

7. Common mistakes to avoid

  • Downplaying how hard things are because you don’t want to complain.
  • Saying “everything is fine” when you’re actually struggling to manage.
  • Not mentioning changes in health, housing, family or mental health.
  • Waiting until the last minute to request reports.

You’re not being “difficult” by explaining challenges — you’re helping the NDIS understand what a safe and sustainable life looks like for you.

8. When to ask for help

If you feel overwhelmed or unsure where to start, you don’t have to do this alone. Many people need support to prepare for reviews — it’s very common.

Able Aide can help you:

  • Reflect on what’s working and what isn’t
  • Gather reports and information from providers
  • Link your goals to the supports you’re asking for
  • Feel more confident talking with the NDIS
