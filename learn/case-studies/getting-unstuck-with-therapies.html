Getting Unstuck with Therapies

How Able Aide helped an NDIS participant whose allied health therapies had stalled — funding was in place, goals were set, but nothing was actually moving forward.

Names and identifying details are changed for privacy.

The situation

"Daniel" was a 34-year-old NDIS participant with an acquired brain injury. His plan included funding for occupational therapy, physiotherapy and psychology — all in place, all agreed on at his planning meeting.

Twelve months later, his Support Coordinator referred him to Able Aide with a quiet frustration: Daniel had attended a handful of OT sessions, had a psychology intake, and was still on a physio waitlist. His Capacity Building budget was largely unspent, and his goals hadn't shifted.

The therapies weren't wrong — the fit, the follow-through and the coordination were.

What was getting in the way

Wrong provider fit

The OT clinic was large, clinical and impersonal. Daniel had difficulty with unfamiliar environments and needed a quieter, more consistent approach — something that hadn't been discussed at intake.

No one following through between sessions

Therapy recommendations were given verbally. No one was tracking whether strategies were being used, or whether the participant was engaging with the home program between appointments.

Waitlist with no active management

Daniel had been on a physio waitlist for 7 months. No one had chased it, looked at alternatives, or explored telehealth options in the meantime.

Communication gaps

The Support Coordinator wasn't receiving progress updates from providers. Reports weren't being shared between therapists. Each service was operating independently.

What Able Aide did

Reviewed the existing providers honestly

We spoke with Daniel about what felt helpful and what didn't — without pressure to stay with providers that weren't working. The OT match wasn't right. We supported the transition to a smaller, home-visiting OT with experience in ABI and low-sensory practice.

Actively chased the physio waitlist — and found an alternative

We contacted three physiotherapy providers, confirmed current wait times, and identified one offering telehealth sessions within two weeks. Daniel started remote physio the following fortnight while remaining on the original waitlist as a backup.

Created a shared communication structure

We set up a simple shared summary — accessible to all therapists and the Support Coordinator — covering current goals, recent progress, strategies in use at home, and upcoming appointments. For the first time, all providers were working from the same picture.

Supported in-home follow-through between sessions

Support workers were briefed on the OT and physio home programs. Rather than treating sessions as isolated events, we helped Daniel practise strategies during regular support shifts — keeping momentum between appointments.

Reframed the psychology engagement

Daniel had disengaged after his psychology intake — the clinic format felt too formal. We supported a referral to a psychologist who offered home-based sessions. He attended consistently from the second session onward.

Outcomes at 6 months

  • OT sessions attended consistently — strategies actively used between appointments
  • Physiotherapy started within 3 weeks of Able Aide involvement (telehealth, then in-person)
  • Psychology engagement established — attending fortnightly, home-based
  • Capacity Building budget being used meaningfully for the first time
  • Support Coordinator receiving regular, clear updates from all providers
  • Daniel described feeling like his plan was "finally doing something"
"For the first time in over a year, I feel like things are actually moving. Someone is actually keeping track of what's happening."

What this story shows

Therapy stalling is rarely about the participant's motivation or the funding amount. It's usually a coordination problem — wrong fit, no follow-through between sessions, providers not talking to each other, or waitlists that no one is actively managing.

Getting unstuck doesn't always mean changing the plan. Often it means changing how the existing plan is being coordinated, communicated and carried out day to day.
