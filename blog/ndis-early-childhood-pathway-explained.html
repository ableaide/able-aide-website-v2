NDIS Early Childhood Pathway Explained

When you first hear about the NDIS for your child, it can feel overwhelming. There are new words, new professionals and a lot of information to take in.

This guide breaks down the NDIS early childhood pathway in calm, simple language – so you know what to expect and how to advocate for your child.

Able Aide provides gentle, practical support for children and families navigating early childhood and the NDIS.

What is the NDIS early childhood pathway?

The NDIS early childhood pathway is a way for young children with disability or developmental concerns to access support earlier in life. You may also hear it called the “early childhood approach”.

The focus is on early intervention – providing the right help as soon as possible, so your child has the best chance to build skills, participate and feel confident at home, in the community and in education settings.

Who is it for?

The early childhood pathway is generally designed for:

  • Children under around 9 years of age
  • Children with a diagnosed disability
  • Children with developmental delay or emerging concerns
  • Families who have been advised to “watch and see” but still feel worried

Every child and family situation is different. Some children will access full NDIS plans, while others may receive short-term or lighter-touch supports through early childhood partners.

Typical steps in the early childhood pathway

While the process can look slightly different depending on where you live, these are common steps families experience:

1. Noticing concerns

You, a teacher, doctor or therapist may notice that your child’s development, communication, behaviour or participation is different to what’s expected for their age.

2. Talking to professionals

Most families start by speaking with a GP, paediatrician, maternal & child health nurse or kinder/school. They may suggest assessments, monitoring or referral to an NDIS early childhood partner.

3. Contacting the NDIS early childhood team

You can contact the NDIS or your local early childhood partner to discuss your child. They may ask questions about:

  • Areas you’re concerned about (speech, behaviour, motor skills, social skills, etc.)
  • How your child manages at home, kinder or school
  • Any existing reports or assessments

4. Assessment and eligibility

The NDIS will consider whether your child meets criteria for:

  • Early intervention supports, or
  • Access to the NDIS as a longer-term participant

You may be asked to provide reports from therapists, doctors or other professionals. If you don’t have these yet, this can be part of what you work toward.

5. Planning meeting

If your child is eligible for an NDIS plan, you’ll have a planning conversation to talk about:

  • Your child’s strengths and challenges
  • Daily routines at home and in the community
  • What’s working well and what’s hard right now
  • Your goals for the next 12–24 months

You can bring a support person, support coordinator or another trusted person to this meeting.

6. Receiving the plan & starting supports

Once your child’s plan is approved, you can begin:

  • Connecting with therapists (OT, speech, psychology, physio, etc.)
  • Engaging family-centred support at home
  • Accessing community and early learning supports

This is where families often feel overwhelmed – juggling appointments, waitlists and paperwork. This is also where services like Support Coordination and Allied Health Access can help.

7. Reviewing progress

NDIS plans are reviewed regularly. You’ll talk about what’s improved, what’s still hard and whether your child’s supports or goals need to change.

What supports can be included?

Every child’s plan is different, but early childhood supports may include a mix of:

  • Occupational therapy (OT) – for sensory needs, everyday skills, emotional regulation and participation
  • Speech therapy – for communication, understanding, speech sounds and social interaction
  • Physiotherapy – for gross motor skills, strength, balance and mobility
  • Psychology or counselling – for emotional wellbeing and behaviour
  • Behaviour support – for behaviours of concern, safety and structured strategies
  • Family coaching and parent support
  • Support workers – to help with routines, community access and practising strategies

Supports should link back to your child’s goals – not just “more therapy”, but therapy that is meaningful and sustainable for your family.

Your role as a parent or caregiver

You are the expert in your child. The NDIS early childhood pathway works best when professionals and parents work together.

  • Share your observations – even if you’re not sure they’re “important enough”
  • Ask questions if something isn’t clear
  • Let services know what is realistic for your family’s time, energy and capacity
  • Speak up if something isn’t working or feels overwhelming

You do not need to be perfect or have all the answers – your role is to be your child’s voice and advocate, with support around you.

How Able Aide supports families in the early childhood pathway

Able Aide works alongside children, families and professionals to make the early childhood journey more manageable.

  • Family & Early Support: practical help with daily routines, behaviour strategies and participation at home – see Family & Early Support.
  • Allied Health Access: assistance finding therapists, navigating waitlists and organising assessments – see Allied Health Access.
  • Support Coordination: for families with this funded in their plan – linking services, problem-solving and preparing for reviews – see Support Coordination.
  • Respectful support workers: who understand that every family has its own culture, routines, faith and parenting style.

Our focus is on calm, realistic support that fits around your child, your family and your values.

Questions to ask at your early childhood planning meeting

Many parents aren’t sure what to ask. Here are some gentle, practical questions that can help:

  • “Can you explain this part of the plan in simpler language?”
  • “What supports could help with mornings, mealtimes or bedtime?”
  • “How can this funding be used to support our whole family, not just individual appointments?”
  • “What type of therapists typically support children with similar needs?”
  • “What should I do if the supports we choose don’t feel like the right fit?”
  • “How will we know if the plan is working well?”

You are allowed to take notes, bring someone with you and ask for information to be repeated.

Feeling overwhelmed by the NDIS early childhood pathway?

You’re not alone. Many families tell us they feel exhausted by forms, assessments and appointments before they even start.

Able Aide is here to walk alongside you with warmth, clarity and realistic expectations.
