NDIS Goal Setting Guide

Clear, practical goal ideas to help your plan match your real life and priorities.

Ask for guidance Back to Resources

Why NDIS goals matter

Your goals guide what supports are considered reasonable and necessary. A plan that doesn't reflect your real needs can mean underfunding — or supports that don't actually help. The strongest goals are meaningful, practical and linked to everyday life.

What makes a strong NDIS goal

Meaningful

Reflects what the participant truly wants — not what sounds "right" for a form.

Practical

Connected to daily life, participation and what actually makes a difference.

Flexible

Not locked to one provider or therapy — open to different support types.

Linked to impact

Explains why support is needed — safety, function, wellbeing or participation.

Goal examples (adapt to suit your situation)

These are starting points — use language that fits the participant's voice and real life.

Daily living

  • "I want to build confidence in managing my daily routine and personal care with less support over time."
  • "I want support to maintain my home safely and manage meals and hygiene independently."
  • "I want to develop strategies to manage fatigue so I can stay active throughout the day."

Community participation

  • "I want to increase my confidence accessing community activities and public spaces."
  • "I want to develop social connections that suit my communication style and interests."
  • "I want support to attend appointments and community events safely and independently."

Wellbeing & safety

  • "I want structured supports to improve emotional regulation and overall wellbeing."
  • "I want a consistent team to help reduce stress and adapt supports when things change."
  • "I want strategies to stay safe in the community and at home."

Independence & skills

  • "I want to build skills that help me live more independently in my own home."
  • "I want to develop communication strategies that help me express my needs clearly."

Link goals to evidence

Goals are stronger when backed by real examples. For each goal, think about: how often the difficulty occurs, what safety risks are involved, what can't be done without support, and what would change with the right funding in place.
