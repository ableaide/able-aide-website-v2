Support Worker Guide

How to choose the right support worker and build a respectful, consistent support match.

Talk to Able Aide Back to Resources

What to look for in a support worker

Good support starts with finding someone whose skills and style match the participant's needs and preferences.

Safety & screening

Appropriate checks (WWCC, police check), safe practices and professional conduct at all times.

Communication

Calm, clear and respectful. Keeps families and coordinators informed without prompting.

Consistency

Reliable scheduling, minimal last-minute changes and a stable presence for participants.

Respect for routines

Works with the participant's pace and preferences rather than imposing a rigid approach.

Cultural understanding

Respectful support that fits the household's culture, language and values.

Participant-led approach

Encourages choice and control — the participant's goals and preferences guide how support is delivered.

Questions you can ask

It's reasonable to ask questions before confirming a support worker. Here are some useful ones:

  • "What experience do you have with supports like mine?"
  • "How do you handle changes or unexpected situations during a shift?"
  • "How do you prefer to communicate with families and coordinators?"
  • "What times are you consistently available?"
  • "How do you approach a participant who is having a difficult day?"

Matching matters

A good match reduces stress and makes supports feel smoother over time. Consider personality, shared interests, communication style and cultural background when possible.

If a match isn't working, it's okay to ask for a change — support should feel safe and respectful. Raising it early (rather than waiting) usually leads to a faster, easier resolution.

If you'd like to understand our approach to matching, see Personal care & daily living or Community participation.
