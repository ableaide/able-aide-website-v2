Support Worker Tips for Families

Simple steps to help supports feel calm, predictable and consistent — for participants and workers alike.

Talk to Able Aide Back to Resources

1. Share routines (simple, not perfect)

You don't need a perfect document — even a short list helps. Write down the basics: preferred wake and sleep times, meal preferences, mobility needs, sensory sensitivities, and what helps when the participant feels overwhelmed or anxious.

Workers who understand the routine from day one can settle in faster and reduce transition stress for the participant.

2. Agree on communication early

  • Preferred language and communication style (visual supports, AAC, verbal, written)
  • Who to update — family, coordinator, or both — and how often
  • What counts as urgent (call immediately vs. next shift note)
  • What to do if something changes or goes wrong during a shift

3. Use short handover notes

Even three or four lines after each shift makes a real difference. A good handover note covers: what went well, what was difficult, and anything to watch for next time.

This is especially important when multiple workers support the same participant — consistency across workers depends on shared information.

Download our free Shift Report Template or Daily Support Log from the Downloads library.

4. Keep changes gentle and explained

Where possible, introduce changes slowly — new workers, new schedules, new activities. Giving advance notice (even a day or two) and explaining what's changing can reduce anxiety significantly for many participants.

If a routine change is unavoidable, keep everything else as familiar as possible to reduce disruption.

5. Raise concerns early

If something isn't working — a mismatch, a pattern of lateness, a communication issue — it's easier to address early than after it becomes established. A calm, specific conversation tends to resolve things faster than a complaint later.

See our Participant Rights Guide for information on raising concerns formally if needed.
