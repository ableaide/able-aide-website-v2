How to Access Allied Health Through the NDIS

Occupational therapy, speech therapy, psychology and physiotherapy can make a huge difference in daily life — but navigating the NDIS process, referrals, waitlists and funding rules can feel overwhelming.

This guide explains how to access allied health step-by-step, with simple language for families, coordinators and participants.

What is “allied health” under the NDIS?

Allied health refers to a range of therapists who help participants build skills, independence, communication, mobility and wellbeing.

Common NDIS-funded allied health supports include:

  • Occupational Therapy (OT)
  • Speech Therapy
  • Physiotherapy
  • Psychology
  • Behaviour support
  • Dietitian & nutrition
  • Exercise physiology
  • Assessments (functional capacity, sensory assessments, autism assessments, etc.)

What funding do you need to access allied health?

Depending on your plan, your therapy may fall under:

  • Capacity Building – Improved Daily Living (most common)
  • Capacity Building – Improved Relationships (for behaviour support)
  • Capacity Building – Improved Health & Wellbeing
  • Core funding (some capacity-building therapy can be flexibly used from Core)

If you’re unsure how your plan works, a support coordinator can help interpret your budgets and avoid overspending.

Step-by-step: How to access allied health through the NDIS

1. Identify your goals

Every therapy must link back to your NDIS goals. Common therapy-related goals include:

  • Building communication skills
  • Improving mobility or motor skills
  • Managing emotions or behaviour
  • Increasing independence at home or in the community

2. Decide which therapies might help

Depending on your needs, you may benefit from one or multiple therapists. If you’re unsure, allied health intake teams or support coordinators can guide you.

3. Contact local therapy providers

Most providers will ask:

  • Your goals
  • Your availability
  • Your NDIS funding type
  • Your location (to check travel)
  • Whether you need home visits or clinic-based therapy

4. Check wait times

Many therapists in Victoria have long waitlists. Families often feel stuck — especially when they need assessments for school or plan reviews.

This is where Able Aide’s Allied Health Access can help.

We assist with fast, coordinated access by:

  • Contacting multiple clinics on your behalf
  • Finding therapists with **shorter waitlists**
  • Organising assessments before plan reviews
  • Ensuring reports meet NDIS requirements

5. Begin therapy sessions

Your therapist will develop a plan, monitor progress and review goals with you regularly. They may provide home programs or school-based strategies.

6. Review and adjust as needed

Therapy isn’t one-size-fits-all. You can request:

  • A different therapist
  • More culturally appropriate supports
  • Sessions at home instead of clinic
  • Support to prepare for NDIS reviews

Do you need a referral?

For most NDIS-funded therapy: No, you do not need a GP referral.

However, some assessments (e.g., paediatric or medical) may require paediatrician or GP involvement.
