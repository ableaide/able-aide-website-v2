Preparing for Your NDIS Planning Meeting

A step-by-step guide to help participants and families feel confident, clear and prepared before an NDIS planning conversation or meeting.

You don't need to be an expert — but knowing what to bring and what to say makes a real difference.

First plan or renewal

This guide applies whether it's your first ever NDIS plan or a scheduled renewal meeting.

Plain language

No jargon — practical, step-by-step guidance for families and participants.

Works for all plan types

Helpful for self-managed, plan-managed and NDIA-managed participants alike.

Step-by-step: before the meeting

Work through these in the weeks before your planning meeting. You don't need to complete everything — focus on what's most relevant to your situation.

Step 1 — Write down your goals

What do you want your life to look like? Think about daily living, community participation, safety, wellbeing, relationships and independence. Even rough dot points are useful — you can refine them later.

See our Goal Setting Guide for examples and prompts.

Step 2 — Gather your evidence

Collect reports, letters and notes that support your goals. These might include allied health assessments, school reports, carer notes, GP letters, or incident records. Recent and functional reports (showing what you can and can't do) are most useful.

See the Evidence & Reports Checklist.

Step 3 — Prepare a "daily life" summary

Write a short (1–2 page) description of a typical day. Include: what you need help with, how long tasks take, what happens when support isn't there, and any safety concerns or risks.

Tip: Be specific. "Needs assistance with all personal care" is stronger than "needs some help." Mention time, frequency, and what happens without support.

Step 4 — Know what supports you currently have (or want)

List the supports you use now (or would like to use). Include: provider name, type of support, hours per week, and whether it's working. If there are gaps, note them — the meeting is the right time to raise them.

Step 5 — Decide who will come with you

You can bring a support person, family member, Support Coordinator or advocate. Having someone there can help you remember things, take notes, and speak up if needed.

If you need an advocate, the Disability Advocacy Finder can help you find one in your area.

Step 6 — Prepare what you want to say

You don't need a script, but it helps to know the three most important things you want to communicate. Keep it grounded in real life — day-to-day impacts, specific difficulties, and what would genuinely help.

Common phrases that help: "Without support, I can't..." / "This happens every day..." / "The biggest risk is..." / "What would make the most difference is..."

What to bring to the meeting

  • Your written goals (even a rough list)
  • Recent allied health reports or assessments
  • GP or specialist letters (if relevant)
  • Your "daily life" summary
  • Current support schedule or service agreements
  • Your NDIS portal login (if using myplace)
  • Notes on what's working and what isn't
  • List of questions you want answered

Common mistakes to avoid

Being too vague

"I need help" is hard to fund. "I need 45 minutes of personal care support every morning, 7 days a week" is fundable.

Downplaying difficulties

Many participants minimise their needs in interviews. Describe what a bad day looks like — not just the best case.

Not bringing evidence

Verbal descriptions help, but written evidence from therapists, schools and doctors carries more weight.

Forgetting to mention changes

If your needs have changed since the last plan, say so clearly and explain why.

After the meeting

Once your plan is approved, you'll receive a copy through the myNDIS portal. Read it carefully and check:

  • Do the goals reflect what you discussed?
  • Is the funding level reasonable for your needs?
  • Are the support categories correct (Core, Capacity Building, Capital)?
  • Are there any restrictions or conditions on how funding can be used?

If you're not happy with the outcome, you can request an internal review. See the NDIS website for the review process, or talk to a Support Coordinator or advocate.
