NDIS Early Childhood Pathway Explained

When you first hear about the NDIS for your child, it can feel overwhelming — new words, new professionals and a lot to understand.

This guide breaks down the NDIS early childhood pathway in calm, simple language so you know what to expect and how to advocate for your child with confidence.

Support for young children & families

What is the NDIS early childhood pathway?

The early childhood pathway — sometimes called the early childhood approach — is designed to help young children with disability or developmental concerns access support as early as possible.

The focus is on early intervention: providing the right support early in life so children can build confidence, participate and develop everyday skills.

Who is it for?

The pathway is typically for:

  • Children under around 9 years of age
  • Children with diagnosed disability
  • Children with developmental delay or emerging concerns
  • Families who feel something isn’t quite right and want support

Some children receive a full NDIS plan, while others access short-term supports through early childhood partners.

Typical steps in the early childhood pathway

While every family’s journey is different, most experience these steps:

1. Noticing concerns

You, a teacher, GP or therapist may notice differences in development, communication, behaviour or social interaction.

2. Talking to professionals

Families often begin by speaking with a GP, paediatrician, maternal & child health nurse, kinder or school. They may recommend monitoring, assessments or referral to an early childhood partner.

3. Contacting the early childhood team

The NDIS or your local early childhood partner may ask about:

  • Your child's development and behaviour
  • How they participate at home, kinder or school
  • Any assessments or reports you already have

4. Assessment & eligibility

The NDIS will consider whether your child may benefit from:

  • Short-term early intervention supports
  • Or a longer-term NDIS plan

You may be asked to provide reports from therapists or doctors. If you don’t have these yet, that’s okay — it can be part of your next steps.

5. Planning meeting

If eligible, you’ll talk about:

  • Your child’s strengths
  • Daily routines at home and in the community
  • What’s working well and what’s challenging
  • Your goals for the next 12–24 months

6. Starting supports

Your child may begin:

  • Therapy (OT, speech, psychology, physiotherapy)
  • Family-centred support at home
  • Community and early learning supports

Services like Allied Health Access and Support Coordination can help you manage appointments, waitlists and funding.

7. Reviewing progress

Plans are reviewed regularly. You’ll discuss what’s improved, what’s still hard and what supports may need to change.

What supports can be included?

Every child’s needs are different, but supports may include:

  • Occupational therapy — sensory needs, routines, independence
  • Speech therapy — communication, speech sounds, social interaction
  • Physiotherapy — mobility, balance, strength
  • Psychology — emotional wellbeing, behaviour
  • Behaviour support
  • Family coaching and parent support
  • Support workers — routines, practice skills, community access

Your role as a parent or caregiver

You are the expert in your child. Professionals rely on your insight to understand how your child manages daily life.

  • Share your concerns honestly — big or small
  • Ask questions if something is unclear
  • Let services know what is realistic for your family
  • Speak up if something doesn’t feel right

How Able Aide supports families

Able Aide works alongside children, families and professionals by offering:

  • Family & Early Support: routines, behaviour strategies, participation at home
  • Allied Health Access: finding therapists, navigating waitlists, organising assessments
  • Support Coordination: linking services, problem-solving and preparing for reviews
  • Respectful support workers: sensitive to your family's culture, routines and values

Our focus is on calm, realistic support that fits your family.

Helpful questions for your planning meeting

  • “Can you explain this part of the plan more simply?”
  • “What supports can help with mornings, mealtimes or bedtime?”
  • “How can this funding support the whole family?”
  • “Which therapists typically support children with similar needs?”
  • “What if the supports we choose don’t feel right?”
  • “How will we know if the plan is working?”

Feeling overwhelmed?

Many parents tell us they feel confused or exhausted before they even begin. That’s completely normal — the early childhood pathway can be a lot to take in.

Able Aide is here to walk alongside you with warmth, clarity and realistic expectations.
